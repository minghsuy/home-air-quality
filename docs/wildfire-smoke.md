# Wildfire smoke & high-AQI days: the clean-room playbook

When outdoor air turns hazardous — wildfire smoke, a major pollution event, an extreme pollen day — the everyday advice inverts. Normally fresh air is good; on these days, **fresh air is the problem.** This page is the step-by-step for riding out a bad-air spell, especially if you have asthma or allergies.

> **Not medical advice.** If smoke is triggering severe symptoms, follow your asthma action plan and contact your doctor or emergency services. Environmental controls reduce exposure; they don't replace treatment.

---

## The core reversal

On a normal day you ventilate to flush CO₂ and staleness. On a smoke day, **every bit of outdoor air you let in carries the PM2.5 you're trying to escape.** So the strategy flips to:

**Seal up → filter hard → concentrate your effort in one clean room → wait for outdoor air to recover.**

You accept some CO₂ buildup as the price of keeping particles out. That's the right trade during a spike.

---

## Step by step

### 1. Watch the outdoor number

Your outdoor PM2.5 / AQI reading is the trigger and the all-clear. When it climbs into unhealthy territory, start sealing. When it drops back down and *stays* down, you can reopen. Don't rely on how the sky looks — measure.

### 2. Seal the envelope

- Close all windows and doors.
- **Shut off or minimize fresh-air intake**: whole-house fans, ERV/HRV fresh-air modes, trickle vents, bathroom/kitchen exhaust when not actively needed (they pull replacement air in through gaps).
- Set the **HVAC to recirculate**, not fresh-air, with the best filter your system tolerates (see [filter-efficiency.md](filter-efficiency.md)).

Note on ERVs: an ERV is great on clean days (fresh air with heat recovery) but on smoke days its whole function — importing outdoor air — is a liability, and many units include only modest filtration. Know how to reduce, bypass, or heavily filter its intake during smoke.

### 3. Run filtration hard

- Run **every HEPA purifier you own on high.**
- Keep the **HVAC fan circulating** so its filter keeps scrubbing recirculated air.
- If your HVAC filter is modest, a portable HEPA unit covers the gap.

### 4. Build one clean room

You don't need to win the whole house. **Win one room.**

- Pick a room you can close off — ideally the **bedroom** (you spend the most time there, and sleep matters most for recovery).
- Put a **well-sized HEPA purifier** in it — aim for the higher end of sizing so it delivers **4–5 air changes per hour** (see CADR sizing in the main README).
- Keep the door closed; make it the space where the most vulnerable person sleeps and spends the day.
- Verify with an indoor PM2.5 monitor that the room is actually staying low. If it isn't, add purifier capacity or tighten the room.

### 5. Manage the CO₂ trade-off

Sealed rooms accumulate CO₂. Mitigate without breaking the seal:

- Don't over-occupy the sealed clean room.
- If CO₂ climbs uncomfortably high and outdoor air has a lull, brief, filtered fresh-air bursts are a judgment call — but on a true smoke spike, keeping particles out wins.
- The moment outdoor PM2.5 recovers, ventilate to flush the accumulated CO₂.

### 6. Reopen deliberately

When outdoor PM2.5 drops back to clean levels and holds:

- Open up and **flush CO₂** back below ~800 ppm.
- Resume normal ventilation / ERV fresh-air.
- **Check your HVAC filter.** A heavy smoke event can load it fast — assess it by efficiency on the next dirty-enough day (see [filter-efficiency.md](filter-efficiency.md)), not by the calendar.

---

## Prep before smoke season

- Have **at least one HEPA purifier sized for your clean room** on hand — don't shop mid-event when they sell out.
- Stock a **spare high-MERV HVAC filter** (MERV 13 if your system supports it).
- Know how to switch your HVAC to **recirculate** and how to disable ERV/whole-house-fan fresh-air.
- Keep an **indoor PM2.5 monitor** in the clean room so you can confirm it's working.
- Know your **outdoor data source** (nearby AQI station or outdoor sensor) so you can time seal-up and all-clear.

---

## One-line version

**Bad-air day: close everything, run every HEPA unit, retreat to one clean room, watch your indoor and outdoor PM2.5, and reopen only when the outdoor number truly recovers.**
